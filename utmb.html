UTMB Research

During my time at UTMB, I worked in a translational research setting using computational analysis to study how viral infection can shift molecular signals carried in exosomes. My work focused on organizing and analyzing biological datasets, validating patterns in expression changes, and communicating results through figures and presentations.

Introduction

Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) and human metapneumovirus (HMPV) are two of the leading causes of respiratory tract infections in young children and other immunocompromised patients. RSV most commonly causes severe illness in infants younger than 6 months, while HMPV is more often associated with severe illness between 6 and 12 months. MicroRNAs (miRNAs) and Piwi-interacting RNAs (piRNAs) are non-coding RNAs that regulate gene expression. Exosomes are extracellular vesicles that transport biologically active proteins, lipids, and RNAs between cells; during infection, exosomes can also carry viral RNA/proteins and host regulatory RNAs, influencing either viral spread or host defense depending on the virus. In this project, we investigated differential expression of genes associated with miRNAs and piRNAs across exosomes derived from RSV-infected cells, HMPV-infected cells, and non-infected controls.

Tools & Methods

  • Programming: R (tidyverse, ggplot2) for data wrangling and visualization; reproducible analysis scripts
  • Differential expression: normalization/QC, filtering, and comparison of infected vs control conditions (exosome-derived RNA)
  • Data handling: structured CSV/TSV outputs; consistent naming and traceable comparisons across RSV, HMPV, and non-infected groups
  • Visualization: publication-style figures (volcano/heatmap-style summaries), clean labeling for posters/presentations
  • Research workflow: translating biological questions into computable comparisons; iterative refinement with mentor feedback
